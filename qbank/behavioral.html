QTell me about a GenAI project you owned and its impact.SeniorbehavioralSeniorfreq: highBoth
Model answer

Use the STAR structure and lead with impact:

  • Situation — the problem and who it was for.
  • Task — your specific responsibility.
  • Action — the decisions you made and the architecture.
  • Result — the metric you moved (latency, cost, quality, adoption).
Strong senior-level answer

A senior leads with the business impact and a number, owns the tradeoffs they made, and credits the team without hiding their own role.

Likely follow-ups
  • What was the hardest tradeoff?
  • What would you change?
Red-flag answer pattern
Saying 'we' for everything with no clear personal contribution and no metric.
QTell me about a time you were wrong, or a project failed.SeniorbehavioralSeniorfreq: highBoth
Model answer

Pick a real failure and structure it:

  • What happened and what you got wrong.
  • What you learned.
  • What you changed afterward so it wouldn't happen again.
Strong senior-level answer

A senior shows reflection plus a systemic fix — e.g. 'so I added an evaluation gate that would have caught it' — not just 'I worked harder'.

Likely follow-ups
  • What did you change systemically?
Red-flag answer pattern
A fake weakness or humblebrag ('I just care too much').
QHow do you handle ambiguous or under-specified requirements?SeniorbehavioralSenior/Stafffreq: mediumBoth
Model answer
  • Ask clarifying questions to narrow the goal.
  • State your assumptions explicitly so others can correct them.
  • Ship a thin slice quickly, then iterate with feedback.
  • Communicate tradeoffs as you go.
Strong senior-level answer

A senior drives alignment among stakeholders, de-risks the unknowns early, and keeps people informed of the tradeoffs.

Likely follow-ups
  • Give a concrete example.
Red-flag answer pattern
Waiting to be told exactly what to do before starting.
QHow did you influence a technical decision without authority?SeniorbehavioralSenior/Stafffreq: mediumBoth
Model answer
  • Make the case with data or a quick prototype rather than opinion.
  • Listen to and address the other side's concerns.
  • Build consensus instead of overruling.
Strong senior-level answer

A senior frames the argument in business terms and brings a measured prototype or eval result to make it concrete.

Likely follow-ups
  • What if you lost the argument?
Red-flag answer pattern
'I just told them I was right.'
